About Us

Street Urchin Studio is an experience design studio in San Francisco, CA. Founding principals Ryan Burns and Emily Gogol have collaborated on a range of projects exploring the intersections of visual art, technology, and theater.

Our team employs the power of story to create compelling immersive experiences. For each project we assemble an interdisciplinary team of experts with a collective mission to create transformative experiences that speak to the humanity of the individual. Our one-on-one approach with clients allows us to deliver exceptionally crafted experiences, just for you and your guests.

Moonshine Alley

A Speakeasy Adventure

Our website opener tells some of this tale. "We invite you, our special guest, to experience Moonshine Alley! As bootleggers, our club has to be a bit secretive , so we've taken a few precautions in getting you in the door. You'll go on a speakeasy adventure to uncover the secret location of our club and earn our trust. What lovely things await you behind Moonshine Alley's door! Besides adventure, there will be a live bluegrass band and a lecture on the art and science of distillation."

We entertained 70 guests for an evening that was one part story and one part bootlegger's club. We served a special whisky flight and handcrafted non-alcohol cocktail flight. We had interative phone booths that led you on the adventure, a fake still, and ... I won't give away all the secrets!
Our full event page can be explored here at Moonshine Alley .

Matthew Briar

A Collaboration with Epic Immersive

Matthew Briar and the Age of Resurrection is an mazing site-specific theater production by Epic Immersive at History Park in San Jose. As part of their production, we created two vignettes.

A Natural History Museum full of curiosities concerning the magical mineral 'Lazarite'. Many elements were hand-made to create this bespoke museum, and in particular Ryan built out a non-newtonian fluid device with a hand-made case to display 'Lazarite' and it's life-giving properties.

A 'place where weary, down-trodden souls could express their sorrow during hard times'. For us, this means drinking 'hooch' from a fake still and dancing to bluegrass among soft lights under a fig tree.

We are not actors, but we did play 'scientist curators' at the museum, and Ryan pitched in when needed in a scene involving a snow cone machine (see pic).The show ran for three nights, and even in soaking rain, we had a ball working with the crew.


Piper

Sculpture

While at Pixar, Ryan wanted to create something to commemorate a special short film called Piper. The timing for the release of Piper, and the tragic passing of a relative who had a special place in his heart for birds, gave us the time and purpose to create this sculpture. We designed and produced this sculpture on a short timescale, all in-house (literally!) using our bereavement leave and a few weeks of nights/weekends.

Piper is a roughly 6' tall sculpture of a sand piper made of foam, rebar, concrete, paper, and resin. The beak is 'mag safe' and can be safely removed and re-attached because of embedded magnets. The legs are attached to the concrete base, which can be removed from the body for transport. We also hand cast black resin eyes using light bulbs. You'll notice from the photos that we cast concrete on our patio and cut foam etc. in our tiny loft. We always find a way to get things done!

Piper now lives on display as a beautiful sculpture, which we have donated to Pixar Animation Studios.


Totoro

Yes, dreams do come true!

To participate in the 20XX Pixar Halloween, we designed and built a Totoro that would rival the Mickey Mouse character at Disneyland! We took great pains with Totoro's aesthetic, including the face, chest fluff and claws. We also made sure he was incredibly huggable with a plush exterior. The armiture was engineered to provide the necessary strength and also comfort so that Ryan could safely 'be' Totoro for the afternoon.

Totoro now lives on display as a beautiful sculpture, and he still gets hugs and photo ops from time to time. Please contact us if you'd like to see our full suite of photos on the construction of Totoro. Contact Emily
